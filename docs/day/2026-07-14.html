Den hellige familie

Johanna Fosie · 1741 · Radering
SMK — National Gallery of Denmark · CC0

✦ Read about this

Johanna Fosie was among the few women working as printmakers in Denmark in the eighteenth century. She produced this depiction of the Holy Family in 1741.

The work is an etching (radering), a technique in which lines are bitten into a metal plate with acid before printing. The subject—the Holy Family—was a standard devotional theme in European art.

The print is held in the collection of the SMK, the National Gallery of Denmark.

The Road Not Taken

Two roads diverged in a yellow wood, And sorry I could not travel both And be one traveler, long I stood And looked down one as far as I could To where it bent in the undergrowth; Then took the other, as just as fair, And having perhaps the better claim, Because it was grassy and wanted wear; Though as for that the passing there Had worn them really about the same,

Robert Frost · 1916 · Public domain

✦ Read about this

Robert Frost was an American poet known for his realistic depictions of rural life and his command of American colloquial speech. He frequently drew on settings from rural New England in the early 20th century, using them to examine complex social and philosophical themes.

"The Road Not Taken" is characteristic of that method: a plain rural scene—two paths dividing in a wood—becomes the vehicle for a larger reflection on choice. The speaker stands at a fork, unable to travel both ways, and settles on one path over the other.

The poem is often read as a straightforward celebration of individual choice, but its details complicate that reading. The speaker notes that the two roads were worn "really about the same," undercutting the sense that one was truly less traveled. The tension between the decision and its actual difference is central to the poem's effect.
