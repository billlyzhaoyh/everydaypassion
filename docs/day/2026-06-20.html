Chocolate Pot

Tiffany & Co. · 1879 · Silver, patinated copper, gold, and ivory
The Met · CC0

✦ Read about this

Made in 1879, this chocolate pot combines silver with patinated copper, gold, and ivory—a mixed-metal approach that departed from the all-silver conventions of American tableware of the period. The combination of materials and surface coloring reflects the influence of Japanese metalwork on Tiffany design during these years.

Tiffany & Co. is an American luxury jewelry and design house, founded in New York and long associated with sterling silver and diamond jewelry. By the late 1870s the firm had established itself as a leading producer of decorative silver, and its work in this vein earned international recognition.

The pot belongs to the collection of The Met.

✦ Read about this

Gerard Manley Hopkins, an English poet and Jesuit priest, achieved his standing among the leading English poets only posthumously. His chief innovation was prosodic: the concept of sprung rhythm, a metrical system organized by stressed syllables rather than regular feet, which gives his lines their dense, percussive movement. His verse is also marked by vivid imagery, much of it drawn from nature and directed toward the praise of God.

This fragment shows those features in concentrated form. The text is heavily stressed, with accent marks signaling its sprung rhythm, and it relies on alliteration, internal rhyme, and chains of compound coinages ("bush-browed, beetle-browed," "jaunting vaunting vaulting assaulting"). The piling of sound effects enacts the physical force it describes.

The poem reaches for an image to convey a powerful sound or "breath"—a heart-stirring music—testing comparisons: a brow-like billow under a south-westerly wind, surf-thundering seas, and a trumpet's blast, each pressing toward the sublime in nature.
